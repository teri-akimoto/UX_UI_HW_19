Endangered Species Redesign

Problem

The government website shows inconsistencies in UI design and a confusing use of multiple search bars which undersells its amazing database on all endangered species in the US and abroad.


Solution

Bring out the best key features of this website:

  • a unified search engine;
  • "Search Species in Your Area" user flow;
  • ability to save visual information for later use in the field.

Team

Team members: Jerry Vu, Ricky Liu, Hoang Lan Nguyen

My role: UX research, IA & Site remapping, user testing, UI design, high-fidelity protototyping


Tools

Figma, Miro, inVision, Google Drive, Zoom, Discord

User Research

Although our user persona wasn’t based on prior user research or official website demographics, our user was essential to find out what he would do on the website and what would motivate him to visit it in the first place. So who’s our user?
Michael is a carpenter from Mariposa County, California. He loves nature, especially camping/hiking with his wife and dog. He wants to find out what potentially threatened species live in his area so he can hike/camp responsibly.
How can he distinguish what species to look out for and avoid?

Prototyping & Testing

Adaptive design required desktop and mobile view tests.
UI style guide was used for the 5-second test to see how well the direction of the redesign fits with the agency and users’ perception of it.
The lo-fi prototypes envisioned a scientific, data-driven feel. However, I looked back on nature-related website designs and got inspired by the park rangers' uniforms for the UI style guide.

UI style tile

Navigation Changes

Research shows that the navigation requires a more cohesive, intuitive layout:

  1. The header is significantly reduced in height to focus on the page content. Breadcrumbs are now included.
  2. Based on previous UI research, a single search bar is favoured throughout (Beacon News, 2010). Michael can refine his search results after a search is made. All search suggestions appear during his keyword input for a quicker search.
  3. The redundant site map is removed from the footer to focus on relevant information (Contact Us) and links to statements required by law.

Homepage Test

5-second tests were conducted for the homepage of the stylized prototype. The findings from 3 user tests validate the proposed UI design in the following ways:

  1. The color palette successfully reflects the "nature", "earth", "animals" that users associate with the agency.
  2. Clear layout, information hierarchy & navigation.
  3. Biggest impact words - "Endangered Species", "Wildlife", "News".

Changes to UI

The findings led to small but impactful adjustments for a better experience:

  • The spacing in the header navigation tabs and article cards was fixed for better alignment.
  • The pictures occupy more space to give the visual focus.
  • 3 key calls to action and home are kept on desktop & tablet view. All other tabs are hidden in the hamburger menu.
  • The footer is reduced in height to make the external links more visible and reduce scrolling.
  • The external links are colour corrected for greater accessibility.
  • Incorporation of mobile UI elements on the home page, including breadcrumb removal, screen-wide hero image carousel on the home page.

Mobile Usability

Our task was to produce adaptive redesign (desktop > mobile). We found that to achieve this, you still need to reiterate desktop to better reflect mobile view. As a result further changes took place:

  1. Static secondary navigation is replaced with a hamburger button to prioritize content on an individual page and keep the UI consistent across devices. A written prompt is given to discover the hamburger menu on search profile pages.
  2. The hamburger icon and the 4 tabs are combined when the width is smaller than tablet view (use the slide deck to see the snap points).
  3. The breadcrumbs are reduced to the previous and current level of the site map to fit the mobile screen.

Mobile Tests

Based on Michael’s user path to search local endangered species in his area, 5 user tests were conducted on the mobile version. It was important to see how well the navigation worked in mobile view.
The performance indicators show that mobile prototype 1 required work in

  1. Information hierarchy in search results.
  2. Easy to access home button outside of the hamburger menu.
  3. Bigger full view image hotspot and relevance to the species.

Final Product

Our goal was to make the website as user-friendly as possible when using its species search feature and saving visual information for later use. You can switch between the user’s desktop and mobile journey.

Michael starts inputting his county into the search bar. Search suggestions show a "pin" which indicates a specific location. He can also tap a specific profile "Mariposa Lily" or search everything by the keyword.
Once he taps the "pin", he's given a number of results for species profiles in his area.

Hm, Michael wants to refine his results to show only mammals since they are easier to spot. The San Joaquin Kit Fox seems like a large enough mammal to find..

Michael can swipe through the images to get a better look of the fox. The images include a short description of the species. There are 2 ways he can save the visual information:

  1. Tap the download button to quickly save on his device.
  2. Open full view and save the image manually.

Reflection

The UI direction and style guide that aligns with a “brand”, or, in this case, a government agency, were key to bringing the design together into a cohesive user experience.
Adaptive, or desktop-first, design had its benefits of greater creativity in navigation. However, the on-going process of prototyping & testing reveal a mutual dependence of desktop and mobile views.
